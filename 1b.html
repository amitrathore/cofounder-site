A field guide

1B.

Unleash the power of one. A field note on solopreneurs riding unicorns — billion-dollar companies built with AI, agents, and business models that put humans back at the center of commerce.

A unicorn is a billion-dollar company. A unicorn rider is the new kind of solopreneur who builds one — powered by AI and agents, anchored in Effective Humanism, and structured around network business models that are both more inclusive and more profitable than the ones they replace.

The unicorn, redefined.

For most of the last two decades, the unicorn was the trophy of the venture machine: thousand-person teams, ten-figure burn, winner-take-all logic. A billion in valuation required a small army to produce it, and the work itself often hollowed out the very humans it was supposed to serve.

The new unicorn is shaped differently. Same valuation, almost none of the headcount. Built by one founder, a handful of trusted collaborators, and a fluent stack of agents doing the work that used to need twenty people. Tiny in payroll, outsized in outcome. Rocketships of value piloted by one.

The unicorn rider.

The unicorn rider is the solopreneur who chooses this shape on purpose. Not a lone wolf — a conductor. They keep the taste, the judgment, and the direct relationship with the customer. They use AI and agents as instruments, not replacements. They invite other humans into the chain of value as partners, not employees, and they let the network do the scaling that a headcount used to do.

The leverage is real. The cost structure of a one-person company multiplied by the leverage of agents makes the unit economics absurd in your favor. Billion-dollar outcomes stop being a function of how many people you can hire and start being a function of how clearly you can see.

Effective humanism, as a business model.

Effective Humanism is not a values statement. It is a design constraint that shows up in the P&L. Use machines to do more of what is uniquely human — care, curiosity, craft, contact — not less. Build for the human in the chain of commerce: the practitioner, the operator, the maker, the curator, the customer. Pay them. Surface them. Compound them.

Done well, this is not a tradeoff against margin. It is the source of it. Trust lowers acquisition cost. Recommendation outperforms advertising. Aligned networks retain better than captured audiences. Inclusive economics attract the participants that closed economics push away. The most humane design tends, over time, to be the most profitable one.

The unicorn rider doesn't extract from the network. They are the network — and the network is the moat.

Network business models.

A solo operator cannot brute-force a market. They have to organize one. The unicorn riders' weapon of choice is the network business model: a structure where every participant — practitioner, customer, partner, agent — gets a real share of the value they create, and the system gets smarter every time they show up.

These models are inclusive by construction, which makes them durable. They are profitable by design, because the founder is no longer the only worker and no longer the only owner of outcome. Pick a small, valuable market. Build the network. Let it compound. The billion follows.

For the daring.

This future does not belong to everyone. It belongs to the people willing to ride animals that mostly don't exist yet — the daring, the patient, the slightly unreasonable. Courage to pick a market that sounds too small. Resilience to keep going when the agents are stupid and the customers are quiet. Discipline to stay small on purpose, so the outcome can be large.

If that sounds like you, you already know. The work is to begin.
