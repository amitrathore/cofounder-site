1B.

Unleash the power of one. A field note on the AI-empowered future of solopreneurship — and the billion-dollar company built by one.

A new shape of company is becoming possible: one founder, a small cast of collaborators, a fluent stack of agents — and a billion-dollar valuation on the other side. Not from owning a continent. From owning a small, valuable market completely.

Rocketships of value.

For most of modern history, scale required a building, a payroll, and a board. Distribution was a privilege of the well-capitalized. Production was a privilege of the well-staffed. The cost of trying something new was measured in years and headcount, which is why almost no one tried.

AI dissolves that cost. Research, design, code, copy, ops, support, even go-to-market — the work that used to demand twenty people can now be carried by one human, a few trusted humans, and a quiet swarm of agents. The bottleneck is no longer capacity. It is taste, judgment, and nerve.

What this unlocks is not a smaller business. It is a different shape of business altogether: tiny in headcount, outsized in outcome. Rocketships of value piloted by one.

The 1B frame.

1B is shorthand for a billion-dollar company, built by a solopreneur. The math that gets you there is not "boil the ocean." It is the opposite. Find a market small enough that incumbents ignore it and specific enough that you can serve it with love — then own it completely.

A few thousand customers paying meaningfully. A few hundred thousand who trust you enough to come back. A category you defined because no one else bothered. That is the path. The cost structure of a one-person company, multiplied by the leverage of agents, makes the unit economics absurd in your favor. The valuation follows.

The audacity is not in market size. It is in refusing to share the work, the taste, or the upside.

A billion-dollar company no longer requires a thousand people. It requires one, with the right instruments and the right market.

Effective humanism.

The risk of an AI-saturated economy is obvious: software eats people, attention gets strip-mined, and the soul of the work goes missing. Effective humanism is the counter-discipline. Use the machines to do more of what is uniquely human — care, curiosity, craft, contact — not less.

A solopreneur operating from this stance treats agents as instruments, not replacements. The output is leveraged. The intent stays human. The customer meets a person, not a funnel. Small markets reward this; it is what they were waiting for.

Unicorn riders.

This future does not belong to everyone. It belongs to the people willing to ride animals that mostly don't exist yet — the daring, the patient, the slightly unreasonable. Courage to pick a market that sounds too small. Resilience to keep going when the agents are stupid and the customers are quiet. Discipline to stay small on purpose, so the outcome can be large.

If that sounds like you, you already know. The work is to begin.
